Investing Classics: Financial StocksBanks, Float, and the Discipline of Underwriting

June 7, 2026·BigCat's Capital Allocator
Finance is the bloodstream of the economy — and one of the most dangerous arenas in investing: highly leveraged, opaque, and held together by confidence. A good financial company is a compounding machine; a bad one can evaporate in hours. This issue unpacks four blocks — the essence of the banking business, insurance float (the machine where others pay you to invest), GEICO's two lessons over half a century, and why underwriting discipline is the only true moat in insurance.
PRINCIPLE 01 · Banking

The Nature of BankingBanking as a Leveraged Business

Leverage & Confidence
The Principle
A bank is, at heart, credit and maturity mismatch run on extreme leverage. High leverage magnifies small mistakes, and the whole edifice rests on confidence — once it cracks, a run is measured in hours.
Source + Quote
"The banking business is no favorite of ours. When assets are twenty times equity — a common ratio in this industry — mistakes that involve only a small portion of assets can destroy a major portion of equity." — Warren Buffett, Berkshire Hathaway 1990 Letter to Shareholders Banking is not a business we favor. When assets run at twenty times equity — common in this industry — mistakes touching only a small fraction of assets can wipe out most of the equity.
Deeper Reading

Banks borrow short and lend long: liabilities are deposits that can be withdrawn at any moment, assets are loans and bonds that can't be liquidated instantly, and equity is just a thin sliver (often only 8–10% of assets — roughly 10x leverage). The deadliest combination is maturity mismatch meeting confidence: deposits can flee in an instant, but assets can't be sold in one. So what truly determines a bank's fate is not the balance sheet you can see, but the risk-control culture you can't — which is why Buffett buys only the best-run banks, and only at a fair price.

Case Study
At 10x Leverage, How Small Asset Losses Devour Equity

Leverage translates "small mistake" into "fatal" — this is the mathematical root of why banks need risk discipline far beyond other industries.

In March 2023, Silicon Valley Bank (about $209B in assets) collapsed within roughly 48 hours. The root cause: it had parked a surge of deposits in long-duration Treasuries and mortgage bonds; as rates spiked in 2022–23 those bonds racked up huge unrealized losses. When it announced a capital raise to plug the hole, its over-90% uninsured depositors took fright at once — about $42B was withdrawn on March 9 alone. Maturity mismatch plus evaporating confidence produced what was then the second-largest bank failure in U.S. history.

Limits & Checklist

This doesn't mean banks are uninvestable: well-managed banks can compound for years, and Wells Fargo was a Buffett holding for decades. But the reversal lives here too — its 2016 "fake accounts" scandal exposed cultural rot, the risk hardest to detect from outside, and Buffett exited thereafter. Misuse: judging a bank only by price-to-book, while ignoring loan quality and deposit structure.

  • Are deposits diversified and sticky, or concentrated in large, flighty clients?
  • Is there duration/concentration risk on the asset side that rate or credit cycles could amplify?
  • Is management's culture prudent, or chasing returns through aggressive growth?
  • Does the price I'm paying leave a margin of safety for "confidence can vanish instantly"?
The Essence
A bank's moat isn't on the balance sheet — it's in the risk culture you can't see; when confidence evaporates, leverage settles the account by the hour.
Reflection
For a financial institution you follow: if the market discounted its "confidence" by 30% tomorrow, would its funding sources still hold?
PRINCIPLE 02 · Float

Insurance FloatInsurance Float

The Compounding Engine
The Principle
Insurers collect premiums first and pay claims later; the large sums they hold in between are "float" — other people's money you get to invest for yourself. It is the hidden engine behind Berkshire's compounding miracle.
Source + Quote
"This collect-now, pay-later model leaves us holding large sums — money we call 'float' — that will eventually go to others. Meanwhile, we get to invest this float for Berkshire's benefit." — Warren Buffett, Berkshire Hathaway 2009 Letter to Shareholders This collect-now, pay-later model leaves us holding large sums — "float" — that will eventually go to others. In the meantime, we get to invest that float for Berkshire's own benefit.
Deeper Reading

Float's value depends on its cost = underwriting profit/loss ÷ float. If underwriting is profitable (combined ratio below 100%), the cost of float is negative — others pay you to hold the money, and you still earn investment returns on it. But the precondition is underwriting discipline: the moment you cut prices to chase volume and run an underwriting loss, float becomes interest-bearing debt, with the rate hidden in future claims. It does not automatically equal "free leverage."

Case Study

In 1967 Buffett acquired National Indemnity for about $8.6M, igniting the engine. Berkshire's float grew from roughly $39M in 1970 to over $160B in 2023, with underwriting profits in most of the past ~20 years — i.e., negative-cost permanent capital that funded large positions like Coca-Cola and Apple. The miracle isn't the float itself, but the combination: "near-free permanent capital + superb capital allocation."

Limits & Checklist

Float can also be a trap. In long-tail lines (asbestos, pollution, liability), claims may surface decades later; if reserves are badly understated, yesterday's "cheap" float turns into ruinous liability. The classic misuse is treating reported float as free money: float that swells from under-pricing is, in essence, borrowing at a hidden high rate.

  • Is this insurer's underwriting profitable (combined ratio durably below 100%)?
  • Is the cost of float negative, near zero, or above its investment yield?
  • Is the business short-tail (fast claims) or long-tail (high reserve-estimation risk)?
  • Does float growth come from rationally priced underwriting, or from price-cutting to grab volume?
The Essence
Float is not free leverage by nature — only when underwriting is profitable is it negative-cost capital; when underwriting loses, it is high-interest borrowing with the rate hidden in the future.
Reflection
If someone tells you an insurer's "float is growing fast," is your first reaction excitement — or to go check its combined ratio?
PRINCIPLE 03 · GEICO

The Rise and Near-Fall of GEICOThe GEICO Saga

Moat & Discipline
The Principle
Low-cost direct selling is a real moat — yet the same company can nearly die from runaway underwriting and then be reborn through discipline. A moat keeps you from being killed by rivals; it can't keep you from killing yourself.
Source + Quote
"The key to GEICO's success is the rock-bottom operating costs that set it apart from almost all other auto insurers. Low costs permit low prices, and low prices attract and retain good policyholders." — Warren Buffett, Berkshire Letters (the low-cost model, stated repeatedly) The key to GEICO's success is the rock-bottom operating costs that set it apart from almost every other auto insurer. Low costs allow low prices, and low prices attract and retain good policyholders.
Deeper Reading

GEICO (founded 1936) uses a direct-sales model — bypassing agents to sell policies straight to low-risk drivers. Its structural low cost lets it offer lower prices, low prices attract low-risk good customers, good customers bring better loss ratios, which reinforces the cost advantage — a self-reinforcing cost flywheel. Rivals don't fail to imitate for lack of trying; their agent-channel cost structure simply can't copy it.

Case Study

This company runs through Buffett's whole life. 1951: at 20, he put over half his net worth — about $10,300 — into GEICO, then sold a year later for roughly $15,300; had he held to 1995 it would have been worth over a million dollars, a lesson he calls "selling too early." 1976: GEICO, after reckless expansion and inadequate reserving, neared bankruptcy — the stock fell from about $61 in 1972 to about $2 (−97%); new CEO Jack Byrne turned it around, and Buffett invested about $45.7M. 1996: Berkshire bought the remaining ~half it didn't own for $2.3B, taking 100% ownership.

Limits & Checklist

A moat ≠ immortality. GEICO's brush with death came from within — underwriting gone wrong — not from competitors. The low-cost moat preserved the franchise and bought time to recover, but shareholders who held from 1972–76 still lost about 97%. A moat buys "time," not "exemption."

  • Is the cost advantage structural, or just a temporary scale dividend?
  • Can the moat withstand internal risks (management, underwriting, culture)?
  • Am I mistaking "has a moat" for "no need to worry about operational mistakes"?
  • If it suffered a self-inflicted crisis, could the moat hold long enough for it to recover?
The Essence
A moat decides whether you survive long-term; discipline decides whether you survive this year — GEICO nearly died from itself, not from rivals.
Reflection
Recall your highest-conviction "moat" holding: you scored its competitive advantage highly, but did you ever score its "self-harm risk"?
PRINCIPLE 04 · Underwriting

Underwriting DisciplineUnderwriting Discipline

The Only Moat
The Principle
Insurance products are homogeneous, unpatentable, and instantly copyable. The only sustainable advantage is underwriting discipline — willing to let premiums shrink dramatically rather than write a loss-making policy.
Source + Quote
"We will write business only at prices we think are appropriate. If competitors are foolish, we will wait. We are perfectly willing to see our volume shrink dramatically rather than write underpriced policies." — Warren Buffett, Berkshire Letters (on National Indemnity's underwriting discipline) We write business only at prices we think are sensible. If rivals price foolishly, we wait. We are entirely willing to see our premium volume shrink sharply rather than write underpriced policies.
Deeper Reading

The core measure of underwriting is the combined ratio (claims + expenses ÷ premiums): below 100% means an underwriting profit. The temptation: insurance is a commodity, and cutting prices easily grows premiums, while losses surface only much later — so aggressive expansion "looks great" for years, then suddenly blows up. Discipline means daring to let your premium volume collapse in a soft market.

Combined Ratio: 100% Is the Line Between Life and Death

Whether the same float is negative-rate capital or high-interest borrowing comes down entirely to this one line — the combined ratio.
Case Study

Positive example: National Indemnity's premiums shrank from about $366M in 1986 to about $55M in 1999 (−85%) in a soft market — Buffett refused to write loss-making policies and refused to cut the team, holding out until the hard market let premiums rebound. Counter-example: Lloyd's of London, in 1988–92, saw long-tail asbestos and pollution liability policies written decades earlier erupt at once, with cumulative losses of about £8B; thousands of unlimited-liability "Names" were ruined and many went bankrupt.

Limits & Checklist

Discipline has a price too: prolonged shrinkage loses channels and talent, and excessive caution can miss reasonably priced growth. The biggest misuse is treating fast premium growth as a mark of success — in insurance, premium growth that's too fast is often not a moat, but the fuse on a time bomb.

  • Is the combined ratio durably below 100%, or are investment gains masking underwriting losses?
  • Does it dare let premium volume shrink in a soft market? Has it ever?
  • Does its high premium growth come from rational pricing, or from undercutting to grab volume?
  • Has it set limits on catastrophe/correlation risk (one event piercing many policies)?
The Essence
In an industry where anyone can copy the product, the one thing that can't be copied is the discipline to say "no"; fast premium growth is often not a moat, but a fuse.
Reflection
If an insurer's most prominent headline is "premiums up 40%," do you read it as good news or as a warning sign?
Deeper Questions
Banks and insurers both run on leverage and confidence — so why has Buffett long held insurance heavily while growing ever more cautious on banks?
The core difference is the controllability of the liabilities. The "price" (premium) and duration of insurance float are set by the company's own underwriting, and with good discipline it can even be negative-cost long-term capital; bank deposits, by contrast, can be pulled at any moment, must compete to be retained, and sit atop an opaque, cycle-correlated loan book. In one line: insurance leverage is "leverage whose terms you write yourself," while bank leverage is "leverage others can pull at any time."
Float is called "negative-cost permanent capital" — but apply that same logic to a fast-growing young insurer, and where does the risk hide?
It hides in the time lag. Float's true cost is determined by future claims, and claims (especially long-tail) may surface only years later; a company that grows float fast by cutting prices can sustain the "negative-cost" illusion for a long time — until inadequate reserves erupt all at once. Moreover, "negative-cost permanent capital" is the result of Berkshire's decades of discipline, heavily colored by survivorship — it is not the default property of the insurance industry.
GEICO's low-cost moat is structural, yet it still nearly went bankrupt — in investing, which deserves more trust: structural advantage or operating ability?
They operate on different levels. Structural advantages (cost structure, network effects) are more durable and verifiable, inferable from the business model; operating ability rots or revives with management and culture and is harder to see from outside. GEICO shows: a structural moat can "buy time to be reborn," but it can't buy "exemption from mistakes." The pragmatic stance: use structural advantage to screen candidates, then use judgment about management's discipline to decide position size and price — without the latter, even the widest moat can be drowned by your own people.
In the AI era, actuarial pricing is being reshaped by data and models — will this widen the moat of underwriting discipline, or erase it?
Most likely a double-edged sword. Better models let disciplined firms price more precisely; but when everyone has similar data and models, pricing skill itself becomes commoditized and the moat is erased. What's truly scarce was never "calculating accurately," but the discipline to walk away in a soft market — that is behavioral and cultural, something models can't supply. The subtler new risk is model convergence: when the whole industry relies on similar models, a systematic error in assumptions (e.g., underestimating correlation or tail events) gets amplified in lockstep into a new systemic risk.
