Investing Weekly · Week 06

Investing Classics: Damodaran on ValuationAswath Damodaran — The Craft of Valuation

June 22, 2026·BigCat's Capital Allocator
Aswath Damodaran has taught NYU Stern's valuation course for over 30 years and is widely called the "Dean of Valuation." Every year he publishes his own valuations of Apple, Tesla, Uber, Aramco, Nvidia — along with the underlying spreadsheets and every assumption. In his view, valuation is not a math game: "Valuation is a craft, not a science; a discipline of judgment, not the application of formulas." This week we return to Damodaran's four pillars — not to teach you how to compute a "target price," but to clarify the relationship between price, value, and the story you can actually defend.
PRINCIPLE 01

DCF — Principles, Power & LimitsDiscounted Cash Flow as a Discipline

Intrinsic Value
The Principle
An asset's intrinsic value equals all the future cash flows it will produce, discounted back to today at a rate reflecting its risk. This is valuation's first principle. But DCF's power is not in "producing a number" — it is in forcing you to decompose your judgment about a business into testable assumptions: growth, margins, reinvestment intensity, risk premium, terminal value. Each number is a bet, and seeing the bets matters more than seeing the final price tag.
Primary Source

Aswath Damodaran, Investment Valuation: Tools and Techniques for Determining the Value of Any Asset (Wiley, 3rd ed. 2012), Chapters 1–4; the more concise The Little Book of Valuation (Wiley, 2011), Ch. 2 "Power Tools of the Trade." Damodaran's free NYU Stern course videos and his blog at musingsonmarkets.blogspot.com have been continuously updated for over a decade.

"A valuation is a bridge between story and numbers. The discounted cash flow model is not a black box — it is a discipline that forces you to be explicit about your beliefs." — Aswath Damodaran, Narrative and Numbers (Columbia Business Press, 2017)
Deeper Reading

The point of the DCF model is not the formula, but the value-driver structure it reveals: (1) how much free cash flow the company can produce (growth × margin − reinvestment); (2) how risky those cash flows are (discount rate / WACC); (3) how long growth can persist (high-growth period length); (4) what it is worth in steady state (terminal value, often 60–80% of total DCF value). Damodaran repeats two points: first, "inputs are outputs" — changing g from 3% to 5%, or WACC from 9% to 7%, can double fair value. This is not a weakness of the model; the model is telling you "here is what really matters." Second, DCF's limits are honest limits — it demands explicit assumptions, while relative valuation hides equally murky assumptions under the cover of "market averages." The former forces thought; the latter rewards laziness.

— Decomposing DCF Value · Terminal Value Often Dominates —
Case Studies

In June 2014, Damodaran publicly valued Uber: 6% annual revenue growth, 40% steady-state operating margin, addressable market limited to traditional taxis — fair value $5.9B. The same month, Bill Gurley (Benchmark) published a rebuttal: Uber is not a taxi substitute; it is the operating system for on-demand logistics, and the market is the entire city's logistics — $25B+. Two DCFs, two stories, a 4× gap — not a math error, a narrative gap. Damodaran progressively revised upward as Uber expanded (UberX, UberEats, cross-city), arriving at ~$58B before the 2019 IPO (close to the actual $69B). Lesson: a DCF is not a one-shot answer — it is a working document to be revised as facts arrive. Second case: Damodaran's 2024 Nvidia valuation made clear that a 70% terminal-value weighting meant the market was betting "AI compute demand will continue compounding for the next 10 years" — any slowdown signal will trigger violent re-rating. Not a prediction, but clarity about what is being bet.

Counter-examples & Limits

Typical DCF failure modes: (1) Early-stage or cash-burning companies — almost any assumption can justify almost any value (WeWork's 2019 pre-IPO valuation collapsed from $47B to $8B simply by changing the narrative); (2) Cyclical companies — using any single year as the base badly distorts; you need "normalized earnings"; (3) Financial institutions — traditional DCF struggles with float, capital adequacy, regulatory costs; use DDM or excess-return models instead; (4) Commodity-price-sensitive businesses — oil ±$20 determines everything. Damodaran himself has missed: in 2014 he underestimated Tesla's narrative elasticity (insisting on auto-only), and in 2018 underestimated Amazon's pivot in capital returns. DCF is not a truth-prediction machine — it is a tool for organizing your own thinking. Its value is not in "giving you the answer" but in "exposing the assumptions you depend on."

Decision Checklist
  • What fraction of my DCF value is terminal value? If more than 70%, am I valuing the company or betting on a distant steady state?
  • Vary g and r by ±1% each — how much does value change? Sensitivity tells me which variable really matters.
  • Is my "high-growth period length" assumption backed by industry evidence (penetration, competitive structure, tech cycle), or just 10 years by default?
  • Is steady-state ROIC above WACC? If not, the company is fundamentally not creating value.
  • Can I describe in one sentence "the business story equivalent to this DCF"? If I can compute but cannot speak, I may not understand.
One-Line Essence + Weekly Reflection
Essence: "A DCF's value is not in its answer but in the questions it forces you to write down."  Reflection: Pick a company you hold long-term. Write down your assumed "revenue growth / operating margin / reinvestment rate / terminal multiple" for the next 10 years. If you cannot write these four numbers, you bought a stock, not a company.
PRINCIPLE 02

Relative Valuation — Power and PitfallsMultiples Are Not Shortcuts

Multiples
The Principle
Relative valuation (P/E, EV/EBITDA, P/B, EV/Sales) is fast and market-anchored, which is why 80% of sell-side reports and investment decisions rely on it. But its convenience hides three implicit assumptions: that the comparables are truly comparable, that the market's pricing of that group is reasonable, and that current multiples are sustainable. If any one fails, the multiple method moves from "fast valuation" to "fast mis-valuation." Damodaran's core warning: the question is never "is a P/E of 20× cheap or expensive?" — it is "what P/E should this company have?" — and that question takes you right back to the same DCF drivers.
Primary Source

Damodaran, Investment Valuation, Chapters 17–20 on Relative Valuation; The Dark Side of Valuation (Wiley, 2nd ed. 2009) systematically documents multiple-method failures in tech, emerging markets, cyclicals, and financials. His annually updated Industry Multiples database at NYU Stern covers 90+ global industries.

"Every multiple has a story attached to it. It is critical that you tell the story and tie it to the fundamentals. If you cannot, you are using the multiple as a black box — and black boxes are dangerous." — Aswath Damodaran, The Little Book of Valuation (2011)
Deeper Reading

Any multiple can be derived from a DCF. For example, P/E = (payout ratio × (1+g)) / (r − g) — meaning P/E is a joint function of growth g, risk r, and payout. Two companies at P/E 15× can be utterly incomparable: Company A with ROE 25%, g 8%, low risk → 15× is cheap; Company B with ROE 8%, g 2%, high risk → 15× is expensive. Damodaran's discipline is "controlled comparison": (1) find genuinely comparable companies (same industry + same lifecycle + similar capital structure); (2) explain current multiple differences (real fundamental gap or market mispricing?); (3) beware "sector multiples" — anchoring to industry averages drags you up into industry bubbles. Multiples are not a shortcut around DCF; they are a sanity check on DCF.

Case Studies

2000 dot-com bubble: Investment banks collectively abandoned P/E (most companies were unprofitable) and switched to P/Sales, P/Eyeballs, P/Clicks. Cisco at its March 2000 peak traded at P/E ~200, market cap $550B — and looked normal on "industry comparable multiples" (Cisco was at the median of its peer group). Because everything was expensive, relative valuation produced a "looks reasonable" answer. The stock fell 89% from 2000 to 2002 and as of 2024 had not regained its 2000 peak. 2021 SPAC and SaaS rerun: Snowflake's post-IPO P/Sales was ~200×; IB reports wrote "industry comparable companies average 80×, room for adjustment." The stock fell 70% in 2021–2022. Counter-example: at the 2008 financial-crisis bottom, P/E averages fell below 10× (whole groups cheap during panic) — relative valuation produced "looks reasonable" reasons to not buy when the entire group was undervalued. Key insight: relative valuation reflects how the market is pricing, not how it should price.

Counter-examples & Limits

Relative valuation is not useless — it has three real uses: (1) short-term market-sentiment barometer — extreme multiples are loud warnings; (2) reverse-check on DCF assumptions — if your DCF implies P/E 80× while the industry averages 15×, you'd better explain why; (3) communication shortcut — clearer for clients and teams. Damodaran himself uses multiples, but insists "every multiple must come with a story." Common misuse: (1) using "industry average" as a target price (the industry itself may be in a bubble or trough); (2) cross-cycle comparison (using 2007 P/E as an anchor for today is meaningless); (3) when using forward P/E, the denominator (forecast EPS) already embeds future growth — then hunting for "low P/E" becomes self-deception. Underlying principle: a multiple is never an answer; it is a phenomenon that requires explanation.

Decision Checklist
  • Are my "comparable companies" actually comparable in lifecycle, capital structure, and geographic risk — or do they just share an industry code?
  • Is the company's multiple higher/lower than the peer group due to real fundamentals (ROIC, g, risk) or market mispricing?
  • Am I using TTM or forward multiples? If forward, whose EPS forecast, and what is their historical accuracy?
  • What percentile of the past 10 years does the industry's current average multiple sit in (90th? 30th?)? How likely is the whole group cheap or expensive?
  • Can I explain in one sentence why this multiple is reasonable? If not, I'm using multiples to skip thinking.
One-Line Essence + Weekly Reflection
Essence: "Relative valuation tells you what the market thinks; DCF tells you what you think. The gap is where opportunity lives."  Reflection: pick a company on your watchlist and write down its current P/E vs. its 5-year average. Then write whether the difference reflects fundamentals (→ may be reasonable) or sentiment (→ may be opportunity).
PRINCIPLE 03

Narrative & NumbersThe Two-Way Bridge

Story Discipline
The Principle
A competent valuation has two inseparable components: a story you can tell + numbers the story disciplines. Pure numbers (DCF with arbitrary assumptions) are "false precision" — you compute to two decimals, but every decimal rests on sand. Pure narrative ("this is the AI revolution, valuation doesn't matter") is "unmoored fantasy" — any price can be rationalized. The two must constrain each other: story determines what numbers should look like; numbers expose whether story is self-consistent.
Primary Source

Aswath Damodaran, Narrative and Numbers: The Value of Stories in Business (Columbia Business Press, 2017) — a systematization of his 30 years of valuation teaching. The book lays out a full "story-driven valuation" method: first the story, then translate it into numbers, then let the numbers test the story.

"Numbers without a narrative are exercises in modeling; narratives without numbers are flights of fancy. Good valuation is the bridge between these two extremes — and the bridge is built in both directions." — Aswath Damodaran, Narrative and Numbers (2017)
Deeper Reading

Damodaran's five-step method: (1) build the story — what is this company, for whom, how does it make money, what is its moat, what could it become in 5–10 years; (2) stress-test the story — possible, plausible, probable. Telling a story about a 30% probability event is fine, but don't price it at 100%; (3) translate story to numbers — market size → revenue growth ceiling, industry margin → steady-state operating margin, competitive density → ROIC, capital intensity → reinvestment rate; (4) compute the DCF; (5) use the result to back-test the story — if DCF comes out 5× above current price, ask "am I over-optimistic?" If 80% below, ask "what did I miss?" This is a two-way bridge: story constrains numbers, numbers test story.

— Story ↔ Numbers · A Two-Way Feedback Loop —
Case Studies

Uber 2014–2019 narrative evolution: In June 2014 Damodaran published "Uber Isn't Worth $17 Billion," using a "taxi substitute" story to compute $5.9B. Bill Gurley publicly rebutted: "You've mis-sized the market — Uber is not a taxi substitute; it's the operating system for on-demand logistics." No mathematical dispute, just a narrative gap — Damodaran's market estimate was $100B; Gurley's was $450B+. As Uber expanded into food delivery (UberEats), freight (Uber Freight), and autonomous driving, the story really did widen. Damodaran publicly revised: 2014 $5.9B → 2015 $23.4B → 2017 $46B → 2019 pre-IPO $58B. He admitted: "I was wrong about the story, but I was right that the story has to be told clearly and constrained by numbers." This is the power of narrative discipline — it doesn't guarantee you're right, but it guarantees you know where you were wrong when you are. Counter-example: WeWork 2019: the S-1 used a "lifestyle + community + tech company" narrative to justify $47B — but the numbers crumbled instantly: revenue $1.8B, loss $1.6B, growth far outpacing sustainable economics. Narrative completely unconstrained by numbers; IPO pulled, valuation shrank to $8B in nine months.

Counter-examples & Limits

Common misuse: (1) using grand narrative as a substitute for valuation — "this is the AI era's electricity" is a story, not a valuation. Ask "what's the margin and ROIC of electricity providers? Can this company match?" to enter the numbers; (2) using numerical precision to fake certainty — DCF says $147.32/share, but every assumption has 30% error, so the real range is $90–$220; (3) narrative consistency bias — the initial story was wrong, but because you've already bought in, you unconsciously use new data to reinforce the old story instead of updating it. Damodaran's prescription: "force-restate the story after every quarterly report and ask whether it was confirmed or refuted." A deeper trap: narratives are social — when friends, media, and analysts collectively tell the same story, independent story-testing becomes extremely hard. That's why Damodaran always publishes his spreadsheets — exposing assumptions to criticism is the only defense against "collective narrative drift."

Decision Checklist
  • Can I tell this company's story in 5 sentences? (Business, customer, moat, end-state, key risk)
  • What are the key numerical metrics implied by my story? Revenue growth, margin, ROIC, reinvestment — do these four numbers actually match my story?
  • Is my story possible, plausible, or probable? Am I pricing a 20%-probability outcome at 100%?
  • After new quarterly or industry news, do I force-restate the story? Or do I just check whether numbers "confirm" my prior view?
  • If the story turns out to be wrong (core assumption falsified), what signal will tell me? Write down exit conditions.
One-Line Essence + Weekly Reflection
Essence: "Valuation is not calculating the future — it is expressing a belief about the future, and letting that belief submit to the discipline of numbers."  Reflection: pick a stock you bought recently. Write down "why I bought it" as a 5-sentence story. Then ask: what numerical assumption corresponds to each sentence? If you cannot match them, you bought a narrative, not a company.
PRINCIPLE 04

Growth vs Value ValuationA False Dichotomy

Concept Clarity
The Principle
"Growth stocks vs value stocks" is a financial-industry marketing phrase, not a real valuation taxonomy. Damodaran insists: growth and value are not opposing choices — growth is an input to valuation; value is the output. Every valuation runs through the same DCF framework; the only difference is whether value comes from the company's existing cash flows (mature companies) or from future growth opportunities (growth companies). The practical consequence: you are not "choosing a style," you are judging the credibility of different sources of value.
Primary Source

Damodaran, Investment Valuation, Chapter 12 "Estimating Growth"; many blog posts and NYU lectures address the growth/value conceptual confusion. Representative paper: "Growth Investing: Betting on the Future" (NYU Stern Working Paper, 2012).

"Growth and value investing are not opposing investment strategies; they are the two ingredients in every valuation. Every investor is a value investor, in the sense that they want to pay less than what the asset is worth. The disagreement is about where that worth comes from." — Aswath Damodaran, NYU Stern Lecture (2014)
Deeper Reading

Decompose any company's value into two parts: assets in place + growth assets. (1) Mature value stocks (Coca-Cola, Procter & Gamble): 90% of value from existing business; growth is the icing. Easy to verify (cash flows already exist), low risk, limited upside; (2) Growth stocks (early SaaS, biotech, AI platforms): 60–80% of value from unrealized future growth. Hard to verify (numbers not yet visible), high risk, open upside. Key insight: growth itself does not create value — only growth with ROIC > cost of capital creates value. If a company reinvests heavily but ROIC sits below WACC, faster growth destroys more value (Bed Bath & Beyond, WeWork, bike sharing, fresh-food e-commerce, and many late-2010s "growth" retail/mobility companies died this way). Damodaran calls this "value-destroying growth" — growth is not free; every unit of growth must answer "is it worth the capital it consumed?"

Case Studies

Buffett's Coca-Cola 1988–present: archetypal mature value stock. When Buffett bought in 1988 P/E was ~15, ROIC sustained at 25%+, with a moat built of global distribution + brand + float-like distributor terms. Key: he wasn't buying "growth"; he was buying sustainable existing cash flows + slow global penetration. 1988–2023 cumulative return ~30× (with dividends), most of it earned in the first 15 years — afterward returns were "preserved by compounding," not "contributed by growth." This is the typical shape of mature value. Contrast: Snowflake 2020 IPO — archetypal growth stock. At IPO, P/E was inapplicable (loss), EV/Sales ~200×. Narrative: cloud data warehousing is the core of enterprise IT for the next decade. But the narrative's "growth" must be tested against ROIC — Snowflake's 2020–2024 revenue grew 70%+ annually, but customer acquisition cost stayed high, operating margin negative, net cash flow zero. Even if the story is exactly right (market expands to $500B), the stock still fell 70% — because the initial price had already discounted 10 years of growth, and growth itself was destroying value (high CAC + low ROIC). Third case: Apple's 2014–2024 transition — Buffett began heavily buying Apple in 2016, but he bought "Apple as a consumer product company," not "Apple as a growth stock." P/E was ~12, ROIC 35%, vast free cash flow buying back stock. Apple had been a "tech stock" Munger and Buffett avoided for years, but during 2014–2018 it transitioned from growth narrative to mature value narrative — Buffett was waiting for exactly that turn. This shows: growth vs. value is not a property of the company, but a phase in the company's lifecycle.

Counter-examples & Limits

Common misuse: (1) "low P/E = value, high P/E = growth" — wrong. That's just the surface. Low P/E may be cheap or a value trap; high P/E may be reasonable or a bubble. The distinction lies in ROIC and growth sources; (2) using "style" as an excuse to avoid thinking — "I'm a value investor so I don't look at tech" is laziness. Buffett's late-life Apple position proves real value investors follow ROIC, not labels; (3) treating "growth" as "revenue growth" — revenue growth + negative ROIC equals value destruction. What matters is "growth that creates value" (ROIC > WACC); (4) using factor models to replace thinking — MSCI Value Index and Growth Index are mechanical classifications. One reason "value factor" underperformed during 2010–2020 is that it mechanically classified low-P/B accounting stocks as value, ignoring intangibles like software and brand; (5) ignoring lifecycle — Microsoft in 2000 was a growth stock, in 2010 a stalled stock, post-2017 a growth stock again (cloud transition). The same company at different stages needs different valuation frameworks. Damodaran's prescription: forget style labels; always ask "where does value come from, and does growth create value?"

Decision Checklist
  • What's roughly the split between "assets in place" and "growth opportunities" in this company's value? (30/70? 80/20?) Does my research effort match the split?
  • Is its ROIC rising, stable, or falling over the past 5 years? Is it above WACC? Growth below WACC is negative value.
  • If it's a growth stock: are the unit economics clear? Can I compute CAC, retention, LTV?
  • If it's a value stock: is the business in steady state or slow decline? Does the discount rate cover the decay speed?
  • Where is this company in its lifecycle? Does my valuation framework fit this stage?
One-Line Essence + Weekly Reflection
Essence: "There is no 'growth stock vs value stock' — only different sources of value + the same DCF discipline."  Reflection: plot the companies in your portfolio on a spectrum from "value from existing assets" to "value from future growth." Which end are you spending most of your research time on? Does that match your actual circle of competence?

DEEP QUESTIONS

Deep QuestionsOpen Questions for the Long Investor

Reflection
1. Is DCF still effective in the AI era, or is any 10-year cash flow forecast now fantasy?
DCF's core mechanism — discounting future cash flows to today — is unchanged in the AI era; what changes is that forecast uncertainty rises sharply. Damodaran's response is "use scenarios, not a single valuation" — give three stories (optimistic / base / pessimistic), compute DCF for each, see where the weighted combination lands. This is not DCF failing; it is honest acknowledgment of structural limits to our knowledge of the future. AI does not obsolete DCF — it makes DCF more necessary, because it is the only framework that forces you to write down assumptions, which matters especially in an era of narrative drift. Damodaran also warns: AI companies have 80–90% of value in terminal value, making valuation highly sensitive to "whether AI can really build businesses with sustainable ROIC > WACC" — a judgment where technically minded investors have a marginal edge.
2. If relative valuation is misleading during industry bubbles, why does the market still rely on it?
Three reasons: (1) cognitive economy — multiples produce results in 5 minutes; DCF takes 5 hours. In an information-saturated era, saving time beats accuracy; (2) career incentives — sell-side analysts using "industry comparable multiples" face less career risk (agreeing with peers = not isolated). "Being wrong with the crowd" is professionally safer than "being right alone"; (3) client preference — clients prefer the certainty of "target price $187" to the honesty of "value range $90–$220." Damodaran's prescription: "Multiples can be used, but always paired with DCF as a reverse check, and valuation should always be expressed as a range, not a point." Individual investors' advantage over institutions is here — you do not need to tell "apparently certain" stories for career survival; you can honestly embrace the range.
3. Can a "good story" bend the valuation? How do you avoid being persuaded by your own story?
Yes, frequently. This is the inherent risk of narrative-driven valuation — Damodaran calls it the "narrative consistency trap." Three countermeasures: (1) pre-commit exit conditions — before buying, write down "what data would make me admit the story is wrong." For example: "if user growth drops below 20% for 2 consecutive quarters, I will revisit and possibly reduce"; (2) argue against yourself — deliberately write a "counter-narrative" with the same sincerity, arguing why this company will fail. If the counter-narrative is better supported, the alarm should sound; (3) publish your valuation — Damodaran publishes all his valuations because publishing is discipline. If you cannot say it aloud, you may know in your heart it cannot survive scrutiny. In the end: a story is not a scam — a story is a tool for exploring assumptions, provided you let it be tested.
4. Do individual investors really need to do DCF? Or is ETF + risk understanding enough?
Depends on what kind of investor you want to be. If the goal is "beat inflation long-term" — index ETFs + DCA + asset allocation are enough; DCF is over-learning. But if the goal is "really understand what you own" — DCF is irreplaceable. Its value is not in getting every answer right, but in training you to recognize market narrative drift. Even if you ultimately buy indexes, an investor who has done a few DCFs will have a markedly different psychological capacity at bear-market bottoms — because they know roughly where intrinsic value sits, instead of being dragged by price. Damodaran's own advice: "Learn DCF once. Then pick 5–10 companies you genuinely care about and value each one annually. Not for trading — for understanding the market." This is why he has kept his teaching public for three decades — he believes the discipline of valuation should be widespread, because it is the mental shield against market panic and mania.
5. Do software / AI / platform companies need a new framework?
Damodaran's view: no new framework, but new application. The essence of DCF — value = discounted future cash flows — is universal. But software characteristics (near-zero marginal cost, network effects, increasing returns to scale, asset-light) change specific inputs: (1) margin ceiling rises (mature-stage 40–50%); (2) reinvestment rate falls (no factories); (3) terminal value weighting rises (greater persistent growth); (4) but moats are more brittle (technology shifts can destroy whole business models). So software DCF is not "impossible to compute" — it just makes judgments about high-growth-period length and end-state margin more decisive. That's why Damodaran insists "story first" — software companies' valuations depend on narrative credibility far more than traditional industries. New frameworks (real options, scenario weighting, staged investment) are extensions of DCF, not replacements.
