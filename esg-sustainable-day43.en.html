Investing Classics: ESG & Sustainable InvestingWhere Values Meet Prices

July 21, 2026·BigCat's Capital Allocator
ESG is the loudest — and most confused — three-letter acronym of the last two decades. It crams three fundamentally different things (Environmental, Social, Governance) into one score, then gets pulled in turn by marketing, regulation and politics. This week takes no side and shouts no slogan; it works four things through: where the core ESG dispute lies, how greenwashing happens, what real "impact" requires, and how to be honest when values collide with returns.
PRINCIPLE 01

Three Letters, One False ConsensusThe ESG Debate

Category Confusion
The Framework
ESG compresses three dimensions — with different, often conflicting goals — into a single score. The more aggregated the number, the vaguer the information; when a "high ESG" score reliably predicts neither risk nor return, what it measures is often "who fills in the questionnaire best," not who is genuinely more sustainable.
Source · Quote
"ESG is not just a mistake that will cost companies and investors money, while making the world worse off." — Aswath Damodaran, Musings on Markets (2021) The valuation scholar Damodaran's sharp critique of the over-broadening of ESG.
Deeper Reading

ESG's root problem is category confusion. E (carbon emissions), S (labor, supply chains) and G (governance, shareholder rights) frequently contradict each other — a well-governed tobacco company scores high on G and low on E/S; how do you sum that into one number? Worse, the rating agencies disagree. MIT Sloan's study Aggregate Confusion found that the pairwise correlation across six major ESG raters averaged just about 0.54, versus 0.99 for credit ratings. The same company can be an honors student to one rater and a laggard to another. When the underlying signal is that inconsistent, what you buy is mostly the noise of a rating methodology, not real sustainability.

Classic Case

In May 2022, S&P removed Tesla from its ESG index while ExxonMobil sat in that index's top ten; Musk publicly called "ESG a scam." The point isn't who's right — it's what a scoring system that ejects the EV leader and keeps the oil major is actually measuring. The answer: S&P's ESG score weights "how a company discloses and manages ESG risk" far more than "the business's net impact on the planet." Two completely different things, hidden under one name.

Limits · Decision Checklist

ESG isn't worthless — the G (governance) dimension has solid empirical support: bad governance really does foreshadow long-term risk. The problem is lending G's credibility to the fuzzy E and S.

  • In the ESG score I use, are E/S/G viewed separately, or blended into one total?
  • Does it measure "net impact on the world," or "the company's risk-management process"? The two get conflated constantly.
  • Switch rating agencies — does this company's rank flip completely?
  • Do I use ESG because it truly improves risk-return, or just to "look responsible"?
Essence · Reflection
ESG is not one signal but three often-opposing signals forced into an average — and averaging costs you information, it doesn't gain you any.
Take one "high ESG score" holding in your portfolio and interrogate the evidence for E, S and G separately. Pull the three apart — does it still deserve that high score?
PRINCIPLE 02

When the Label Outruns the SubstanceGreenwashing

Information Asymmetry
The Framework
Greenwashing wraps unchanged substance in ESG language: add "sustainable" to a fund's name, pile green imagery into the report — while the holdings, supply chain or carbon footprint barely change. It proliferates because demand (money wanting to be responsible) has raced far ahead of verifiable supply.
Source · Quote
"Investment advisers must ensure that their actions conform to their words." — Sanjay Wadhwa, SEC Enforcement, on the DWS settlement (2023) A U.S. SEC official's statement on the DWS greenwashing penalty.
Deeper Reading

The economics of greenwashing are simple: a "green" label attracts money and commands a higher fee, while the cost of verifying the claim falls on someone else — information asymmetry creates room for marketing arbitrage. The regulatory response is to turn "said" into "must do": the EU's SFDR requires funds to classify and disclose by degree of sustainability; the U.S. SEC enforces directly against false statements. But regulation breeds a new game of its own: once "green" becomes an arbitrageable label, the rules defining the label become the new battleground.

Classic Case

DWS, Deutsche Bank's asset-management arm, once claimed ESG was "in its DNA." In 2021 its former head of sustainability blew the whistle that this was not the case; in May 2022 German police raided its Frankfurt headquarters and the CEO soon resigned; in September 2023 the SEC fined DWS $19 million over ESG-related misstatements. A larger-scale signal: after SFDR took effect, from late 2022 into early 2023 large numbers of funds originally labeled top-tier (Article 9) were reclassified down to Article 8, covering well over a hundred billion dollars in assets — the business hadn't changed; the bar for "green" had finally been enforced.

Limits · Decision Checklist

Not every label change is greenwashing — sometimes it's the healthy result of tighter rules and clearer definitions. Real greenwashing is about the gap between "said" and "done."

  • Is this fund's "sustainability" in the holdings, or only in the name and cover?
  • If you stripped the ESG label from its top ten holdings, could you tell any difference?
  • The premium fee it charges — does it buy verifiable substance, or an adjective?
  • When regulators tighten definitions, does it raise its standards, or quietly reword?
Essence · Reflection
Greenwashing isn't lying — it's letting an adjective speak for the substance. Testing it takes one move: cover the label, look at the holdings.
For any "ESG/sustainable" product you hold, cover its name and marketing and look only at the holdings list — how different is it, really, from a plain index fund?
PRINCIPLE 03

The Test Is AdditionalityImpact Investing

Additionality
The Framework
The touchstone of impact investing is not "I bought a good company" but "because of my capital, the real world gained one more solar panel, one fewer ton of emissions." The concept is additionality. Buying a "green" stock on the secondary market just moves money from one shareholder to another — the company's account gains nothing. That is not impact; it is preference.
Source · Quote
"Impact investments are made with the intention to generate positive, measurable social and environmental impact alongside a financial return." — Global Impact Investing Network (GIIN), definition Note the word "measurable" — it is the crux of the whole idea.
Deeper Reading

You must separate three things that are constantly conflated: (1) negative screening (don't buy tobacco, weapons) — this changes your portfolio, not the world; (2) ESG integration — treating ESG as a risk factor in valuation, still aimed at returns; (3) impact investing — using primary-market capital (venture, project finance, private credit) to do things that would not happen without your money. The first two have almost no additionality in secondary markets: the stock you sell is instantly bought by someone else, and the company's cost of capital doesn't budge. Real additionality lives where capital is scarce — early-stage clean tech, developing-world infrastructure, transition projects nobody else will lend to.

Classic Case

Academia has a sober quantification of this. Berk and van Binsbergen's study The Impact of Impact Investing (2021) found that broad divestment has a negligible effect on the cost of capital of the divested firms — because there is always an indifferent buyer to step in at a slightly higher expected return. Their conclusion: to actually change corporate behavior, engagement (staying invested and pressing) is far more effective than divestment. In other words, selling the stock feels good, but holding it and voting against management — or steering new capital toward what is genuinely starved of it — is what might create additionality.

Limits · Decision Checklist

Additionality is extremely hard to measure and the easiest thing to overstate — nearly every impact fund tells a moving impact story.

  • If I didn't invest this money, would the good thing still happen? (If yes, additionality ≈ 0.)
  • Is this primary market (new capital into a real entity), or secondary (shares changing hands)?
  • Is the impact metric an auditable real-world output, or a narrative the fund tells about itself?
  • Am I pursuing real change, or buying the emotional comfort of "I took part"?
Essence · Reflection
Impact without additionality is just preference under a new name — the real question is always: would this happen without my money?
For any "to do good" investment decision you've made, if you swapped it for "donate the money + buy the cheapest index," which does more net good for the world?
PRINCIPLE 04

Price Your Convictions HonestlyValues vs Returns

Pricing the Trade-off
The Framework
When values collide with returns, the mature move is not to pretend there's no cost, but to price your convictions with clear eyes. Excluding an asset class mathematically must change your expected return — it may fall slightly from lost diversification, or you may "benefit" from others' aversion. What matters is knowing what price you pay, and whether it's worth it.
Source · Quote
"Virtue is its own reward: or, one man's ceiling is another man's floor." — Cliff Asness, AQR (2017), on the sin-stock premium Asness of AQR nails it: the stock you shun and thereby depress is exactly the source of someone else's higher expected return.
Deeper Reading

The logic is clean: if a group of investors refuses on moral grounds to buy "sin stocks" (tobacco, gambling, fossil fuels), those stocks' prices are pushed down and their future expected returns therefore rise — and that premium is earned by the investors who don't care. That is the source of the "sin premium." So values investing inherently contains a trade-off: you exchange (potential) excess return for sleep that matches your convictions. This is not a moral failure — it's a choice that must be honestly priced. What is truly dangerous is the free-lunch narrative that "responsible also earns more" — it dodges the trade-off, and the moment sin stocks outperform, its adherents waver.

Classic Case

In The Future for Investors (2005), Siegel found that among the original 1957 constituents of the S&P 500, the long-run return champion was Philip Morris (tobacco, now Altria), returning roughly 19.75% annualized from 1957 to 2003, far ahead of the index — driven partly by chronically low valuations compounded with reinvested high dividends. The other side: Norway's sovereign fund (GPFG) maintains a long-standing ethics-based exclusion list (tobacco, certain weapons, severe polluters) and openly acknowledges these exclusions have a measurable, usually negative but acceptable effect on returns — it chooses to bear that cost transparently rather than pretend it doesn't exist.

Limits · Decision Checklist

Values investing doesn't have to underperform — diversifying and avoiding genuine long-term risks (e.g. stranded assets) can even help. The key is not to fool yourself.

  • Am I excluding an asset class out of conviction, or out of a prediction that "it will underperform"? (The latter is often wrong.)
  • Would I hold the exclusion even if that asset class beat me for three years straight?
  • Is the (expected) price I pay for my values small enough to bear over the long run?
  • Am I buying the "responsible and more profitable" free-lunch narrative? If so, beware.
Essence · Reflection
Values can guide investing, but they are never free — maturity isn't pretending there's no cost, but knowing what the cost is and being willing to pay it.
Write down one investing line you won't cross (an industry you won't touch under any circumstances). Then ask honestly: if it were the most profitable sector of the next decade, could you hold the line? What is the most you'd pay to keep it?

Deeper Questions

Does ESG investing actually deliver higher returns?
The honest answer: the evidence is mixed and inconclusive. Countless studies reach opposite conclusions depending on method, period and region; the governance (G) dimension has more support, E and S far less. More fundamentally, if "ESG earns more" became consensus, inflows would push up valuations and erode future excess returns — it cannot be both a moral preference and a permanent money machine. Treating ESG as a risk-management tool (helping you dodge governance and regulatory landmines) stands on firmer ground than treating it as a source of alpha.
As a long-term investor, should I ignore ESG entirely?
No need for extremes — take it apart and use it. Governance (G) is an old value-investing topic; take it seriously. Among environmental and social risks, whatever converts into real financial consequence (carbon taxes, litigation, supply-chain disruption, stranded assets) belongs in your valuation; the rest, which can't be quantified into cash flows, is a matter of personal values — price it honestly. What to reject is not ESG, but the marketing version that blends all three into one score and then claims it raises returns for free.
In the AI era, will ESG data become more reliable?
Technology can improve measurement (satellites for emissions, NLP scanning supply-chain sentiment), but it can't solve the root problem: most ESG disagreement is about value judgments (how to weight the three dimensions, what counts as "good"), not missing data. More data may even deepen the "aggregate confusion" — different algorithms process the same data and still diverge. AI will make greenwashing slicker and detection sharper alike; the real guardrail was never the volume of data, but whether the old rule — say what you do, do what you say — can be enforced.
