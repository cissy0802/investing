Investing Classics: Crypto as an Asset ClassPriced, Not Valued

July 20, 2026·BigCat's Capital Allocator
Crypto is the noisiest asset of this era, and the one most likely to strip away your judgment: no cash flows, no moat, its price driven almost entirely by narrative and mood — yet it has gone from worthless to a trillion-dollar market cap in barely a decade. For the long-term investor, the real question is never "bull or bear," but four calmer ones: can Bitcoin actually be valued, what does its extreme volatility mean, who is right in the "Ponzi vs innovation" debate, and how should a rational person position it. This week we won't predict the price — we'll work through those four things.
PRINCIPLE 01

Priced, Not ValuedWhy There Is No Intrinsic Anchor

Value Narrative
The Framework
Bitcoin has no cash flows, so you cannot "value" it with a DCF — you can only "price" it. Its price is set by supply, demand, the scarcity narrative and confidence, not by cash that can be taken out in the future. Confusing "pricing" with "valuing" is the crypto investor's most fundamental error.
Source · Quote
"Bitcoin is not an asset, since it does not generate cash flows... You can price bitcoin, but you cannot value it." — Aswath Damodaran, "The Bitcoin Boom: Asset, Currency, Commodity or Collectible?" (2017)
Interpretation

Valuation asks "how much cash will this asset produce over its remaining life, and what is that worth discounted" — stocks have earnings, bonds have coupons, property has rent; Bitcoin has none of the three. So it falls into what Damodaran calls the "can be priced, cannot be valued" category, alongside gold and fine art. This doesn't mean it can't rise — a priced asset can absolutely climb because more people believe in it. But it means you have no intrinsic-value anchor. With a stock you can say "price is below value, so there's a margin of safety"; with Bitcoin you can only say "I'm betting someone will take it off my hands at a higher price." Separating these two acts is where discipline begins.

Case Study

The "digital gold" narrative is real and powerful: Bitcoin's supply is hard-capped at 21 million, its scarcity programmable and verifiable — a precision even gold lacks. In 2020–2021 MicroStrategy and Tesla put it on their balance sheets, in 2021 El Salvador made it legal tender, and in 2024 US spot ETFs were approved — the narrative institutionalized step by step. But that same "scarcity" story cannot explain why the price can halve and halve again within a single year. Scarcity determines that it "won't be diluted" — not how much it is "worth."

Limits · Decision Checklist

Conversely, "no cash flow means worthless" is too glib — gold has generated no cash flow for millennia yet has always been a store of value. The point isn't cash flow or none, but whether you're clear that you are pricing, not valuing, and size your bet accordingly.

  • Am I buying because I estimated intrinsic value, or because I believe someone will pay more?
  • Can I articulate which demands drive its price, and how those demands might change?
  • Am I using the "margin of safety" illusion of stock-buying to disguise what is really a pricing game?
Essence · Reflection
Bitcoin can be priced but not valued — before you buy, honestly admit which one you are doing.
The crypto you hold or want to buy: if you could never sell it to "the next person," would it still have value to you? The answer reveals whether you are investing or speculating.
PRINCIPLE 02

Path, Not Just VolatilityPosition Size Is the Only Defense

Volatility & Allocation
The Framework
Bitcoin's volatility is several times that of stocks, and it has repeatedly suffered 80%-scale drawdowns. For an asset with no cash flow — one that can't "grow its way back" through earnings — a drawdown that deep may be permanent. The real risk isn't volatility itself, but path: will the price hit zero before you get shaken out or margin-called?
Source · Quote
"Never cross a river if it is on average four feet deep." — Nassim Taleb, "Skin in the Game" (2018)
Interpretation

Taleb uses this to separate "ensemble probability" from "time probability": a river four feet deep on average may be ten feet deep somewhere — and once you drown once, the "average" is meaningless. Crypto is exactly like this: the astonishing long-run returns on the chart belong only to those never zeroed out by some intervening 80% drawdown or exchange blowup. Volatility measures the bumps; what actually kills you is the irreversible trip to zero. For this kind of asset, position size is the only real risk control — you can't predict the price, but you can decide "the most I can lose." And the math of drawdowns is brutal: the deeper the fall, the more exponentially the gain needed to recover.

DrawdownGain Needed to Recover
−50%+100%
−80%+400%
−90%+900%
Case Study

In December 2017 Bitcoin touched about $19,800, and a year later had fallen to roughly $3,200 (−84%); in November 2021 it hit about $69,000, and by November 2022 had dropped below $16,000 (−77%). Alongside came a string of "zeroes": Mt. Gox collapsed in 2014 having lost some 850,000 bitcoin; in May 2022 the Terra/LUNA algorithmic stablecoin vaporized about $40 billion in days; that November the FTX exchange imploded. Anyone using leverage or fully allocated could have been permanently wiped out at any one of them.

Limits · Decision Checklist

Conversely, precisely because volatility is extreme, a tiny position can deliver meaningful convexity — which is the subject of Card 4. Volatility itself isn't the enemy; the wrong position size is.

  • If this investment went to zero tomorrow, would my life and portfolio take real damage?
  • Is my position small enough that I won't be forced to sell — or lose sleep — in an 80% drawdown?
  • Am I using spot or leverage? Leverage turns "volatility" directly into "zero."
Essence · Reflection
Volatility measures the bumps; zero measures the end. What keeps you alive is never your judgment — it's your position size.
Suppose your crypto position halves and halves again tomorrow — would you panic-sell, or not flinch? The answer tells you whether your position today is too big.
PRINCIPLE 03

Ponzi or InnovationSeparate the Tech From the Token Price

Nature of the Debate
The Framework
The fiercest debate about crypto: is it a speculative bubble driven by the greater fool, or a misunderstood technological innovation? The honest answer is that both can be true at once. The underlying technology (decentralized, censorship-resistant settlement) is genuine innovation, while the frenzy around token prices often looks like a Ponzi-style game of pass-the-parcel. Separating "the technology" from "the token price" is the key.
Source · Quote
"Bitcoin is probably rat poison squared." — Warren Buffett, Berkshire Hathaway annual meeting (2018)
Interpretation

Buffett and Munger's critique cuts deep: an asset that produces nothing and can only hope to be sold on at a higher price rises in a way structurally identical to a Ponzi — early entrants' gains come from later entrants' principal; Munger flatly called it "stupid, evil, and makes me look bad." But at the other end, Howard Marks offered rare humility: he dismissed crypto as a speculative bubble in 2017, then publicly revised course in his 2021 memo "Something of Value" — under his son Andrew's influence, admitting he had "been too quick to dismiss it," and that the technology itself might create real value. True wisdom is to be neither swept up by the frenzy nor, out of contempt, refuse to understand.

Case Study

Both sides have hard evidence. Innovation: blockchain enables value transfer without trusting a third party, and stablecoins have become a real dollar channel in some high-inflation, capital-controlled countries. Bubble: in the 2017 ICO mania, countless product-less tokens raised billions on a whitepaper and mostly went to zero; the 2021 meme-coin frenzy and the 2022 collapses of Terra and FTX were textbook pass-the-parcel. Within the same industry, real innovation and pure speculation coexist — declaring blanketly "crypto is a scam" or "crypto is the future" is just lazy.

Limits · Decision Checklist

Beware two extremes: treating the whole industry as a scam makes you miss the genuine technical progress; treating it as "the inevitable future" makes you overpay for any token. The only way to tell them apart is to see through the price to the real use.

  • Am I buying a technology/network with real utility, or a token propped up only by "it goes up"?
  • Does this project's return come from real economic activity, or from later buyers coming in?
  • Can I, like Marks, stay skeptical yet be willing to truly understand it before concluding?
Essence · Reflection
The blockchain may be innovation and a given token may be a Ponzi — look at the industry and the price separately, so you neither miss the innovation nor step on the scam.
For a crypto project you follow: strip away the expectation that "the price will rise" — what real-world problem does it still solve?
PRINCIPLE 04

Sizing the Bet: Size Over TimingThe Barbell

Portfolio Positioning
The Framework
If a rational person is to participate in crypto, the only defensible way is usually the "barbell": keep the vast majority of assets in a safe, valuable core, and use only a small slice of money you can afford to lose entirely to take a high-convexity, asymmetric crypto exposure. Position size, not entry timing, is the most important decision here.
Source · Quote
"I have just over 1% of my assets in bitcoin. Maybe it's almost 2. That seems like the right number right now." — Paul Tudor Jones, CNBC / BVI memo (2020–2021)
Interpretation

This synthesizes the first three cards into a single decision. Since Bitcoin can be priced but not valued (Card 1), can go permanently to zero (Card 2), and mixes real innovation with pure speculation (Card 3), a rational exposure must meet two conditions: small enough that a total loss doesn't hurt, large enough that if it truly becomes "digital gold" you don't miss it entirely. The legendary macro trader Paul Tudor Jones likened Bitcoin to "gold in 1976," yet gave it only a 1–2% position — believing in its asymmetric upside while being fully aware of its zero risk. This is the Taleb barbell: a tiny, capped bet to capture a large, open-ended upside.

Case Study

The cautionary cases are just as clear. In 2021 Tesla put $1.5 billion into Bitcoin; months later it took an impairment as the price fell and sold about 75% of its holding — for a cash-strapped company, a position like that could be fatal. El Salvador bet on Bitcoin with the national balance sheet, exposing a country's finances to a single high-volatility asset. Against Paul Tudor Jones's 1–2%: with the same asset, position size decides whether it is "a bearable convex bet" or "a risk source that can blow through you."

Limits · Decision Checklist

The barbell has a precondition too: that "small position" must be money you can genuinely lose entirely — not retirement, home-purchase, or a child's education money — and you must resist the bull-market temptation of "I should have bought more" that keeps you adding. That is exactly how a barbell degenerates into being all-in.

  • Is my crypto position small enough that if it zeroed tomorrow I'd still sleep, and life unchanged?
  • Is this money I can afford to lose, or capital earmarked for a specific need?
  • In the bull market, am I quietly breaching my own ceiling?
  • Is the core at the other end of the barbell robust enough and valuable?
Essence · Reflection
For an asset that could go to zero or 10x, what decides your fate isn't how accurate you are — it's how much you dare to bet.
Write down the maximum percentage you'd allocate to crypto, and honestly ask: is that number based on "how much I can afford to lose," or on "how much I hope to make"?

Going Deeper

Should a long-term value investor avoid crypto entirely?
Buffett would say avoid it entirely: an asset that generates no cash flow and cannot be valued lies outside the scope of value investing — logically airtight. But the finer answer depends on how you define "investing": if you strictly buy only assets you can value, crypto is inherently outside your circle of competence, and avoidance is discipline, not cowardice; if you view a tiny position as a "tail hedge" against the fiat system (not a core holding), it can live at the extreme end of a barbell. The key is not to fool yourself — don't use the language of value investing to dress up what is a pure pricing game. Both avoidance and a small position can be rational; only "large position while claiming to do value investing" is not.
To allocate, should I buy a spot ETF, hold coins directly, or buy "crypto-proxy stocks"?
The three carry very different risks. A spot ETF (approved in the US in 2024) solves custody and security, best suited to ordinary investors who just want price exposure — but mind the management fee and the fact that it only tracks price and pays no cash flow. Holding coins directly gives you full autonomy but loads all the key-security and exchange-blowup risk (see FTX) onto you. "Crypto-proxy stocks" (miners, exchanges) add operating leverage and equity dilution, and often swing more than Bitcoin itself — they are a leveraged bet on the price, not a substitute. There's no optimal answer, only the one that matches your risk tolerance.
In the AI era, will crypto's narrative be rewritten?
A new demand-side narrative is indeed emerging: some argue that automated payments between AI agents and on-chain settlement need a "machine-native currency," which could bring real use cases. But run it back through this week's frame with two questions: first, is this a real technical need, or another "this time it's different" top-of-cycle story? Second, even if the demand is real, has the market already paid for it at an extreme price? The more worrying reverse risk is that AI drives the cost of hype, pumps and scams toward zero — which could inflate crypto's "Ponzi" side faster than its "innovation" side. New technology never automatically equals new value.
