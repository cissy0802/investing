Investing Weekly · Week 01

Investing Classics: Buffett's Core PrinciplesThe Bedrock of Value Investing

May 20, 2026·BigCat's Capital Allocator
Warren Buffett's sixty-year record was not built on predicting markets. Stripped to the bone, his thinking comes down to four things: know your boundaries, buy businesses with moats, use Mr. Market rather than be driven by him, and measure decisions by value rather than price. This week we go back to the source on these four foundations.
PRINCIPLE 01

Circle of CompetenceCircle of Competence

Cognitive boundaries
Statement of the Principle
Investing success comes not from how much you know, but from whether you can cleanly distinguish what you understand from what you don't — and only bet inside the former.
Source

Buffett's 1996 letter to shareholders, echoing the 1992 letter.

"What an investor needs is the ability to correctly evaluate selected businesses. Note that word 'selected': you don't have to be an expert on every company, or even many. You only have to be able to evaluate companies within your circle of competence. The size of that circle is not very important; knowing its boundaries, however, is vital." — Berkshire Hathaway 1996 Letter to Shareholders
Deeper Reading

The circle of competence is not a showcase for knowledge — it is a defensive perimeter. The core task of valuation is forming a probabilistic view of a business's future cash flows. Inside your circle, you have a reasonable prior on that distribution; outside it, you are guessing. Buffett's emphasis falls on "knowing the boundary" rather than "expanding the circle." An investor who truly understands three industries and stays inside them will, over time, beat the apparently better-read generalist who touches everything. As Munger puts it, you should "play in the games where you have an edge."

Classic Case

At the peak of the dot-com bubble in 1999, Buffett's refusal to buy tech stocks earned him the Barron's cover "What's Wrong, Warren?" A year later the Nasdaq fell from 5,048 to 1,114 (-78%), and thousands of companies went to zero. Buffett openly admitted, "I don't understand what these companies' cash flows look like next year," and stepped aside from a disaster that swallowed enormous quantities of "smart money." Berkshire's book value continued to grow.

Counterexamples and Limits

The circle is not static; it can be expanded through deep study (Buffett's later Apple investment is an example — but he reached it through the familiar lens of "consumer brand + customer stickiness," not by judging the technology). Common misuses: (1) treating "I have owned this" as "I understand this"; (2) opportunistically widening the boundary during a bull market; (3) using "outside my circle" as an excuse not to learn. A real circle of competence is dynamic, verifiable, and explainable to a 12-year-old in a paragraph.

Decision Checklist
  • Can I explain in three sentences how this company makes money — to a layperson?
  • Can I reasonably estimate the range of its earnings five years out (not the precise number)?
  • Do I have an independent view on the core variables (regulation/technology/competition) of this industry?
  • If the market closed for five years tomorrow, would I still want to own this?
  • Is this inside the circle or on its edge? Has the edge-of-circle position size been reduced accordingly?
English Insight

"Knowing what you don't know is more useful than being brilliant." — Charlie Munger

Reflection This Week
List every position currently in your portfolio and label each one as "core (inside the circle)," "edge of circle (still learning)," or "outside the circle (speculation)." Does the sum of "outside" positions exceed the maximum permanent loss you can stomach?
PRINCIPLE 02

Economic MoatEconomic Moat

Competitive advantage
Statement of the Principle
A great business must have a wide and deep moat that protects its high returns on capital from being eroded quickly by competition.
Source

Buffett's 1995 letter, systematized in the 2007 letter.

"A truly great business must have an enduring 'moat' that protects excellent returns on invested capital. The dynamics of capitalism guarantee that competitors will repeatedly assault any business 'castle' that is earning high returns." — Berkshire Hathaway 2007 Letter
Deeper Reading

The essence of a moat is: "Why can't competitors easily take your profits?" Common sources: (1) intangible assets (brand, patent, license); (2) switching costs (enterprise ERP, primary banking relationship); (3) network effects (social platforms, payment networks); (4) cost advantages (scale, geography, exclusive resources); (5) efficient scale (monopoly of a small market). What Buffett cares about is not "how much it earns now" but "whether it can still earn that much in ten years." High ROIC plus durability is the actual compounding machine. A 25%-ROIC business losing 2 points per year to competition has far less long-term value than an 18%-ROIC business that holds the line.

Classic Case

See's Candies (acquired by Berkshire in 1972 for $25 million): the book net assets were only $8 million, so the price looked threefold expensive. But Buffett saw the brand's pricing power — See's could raise prices every year without losing volume. By 2007, See's had delivered more than $1.35 billion in cumulative pre-tax profit to Berkshire, against incremental capital invested of less than $40 million. The textbook case of an asset-light moat built on pricing power. Buffett called the deal "the one that changed our investment philosophy" — moving from cigar-butt stocks to quality businesses.

Counterexamples and Limits

Moats get disrupted by technology: Kodak's brand-and-distribution moat dissolved against digital photography; Blockbuster's store network was destroyed by streaming. Common misuses: (1) equating "high market share" with a moat (share is an outcome, not a cause); (2) mistaking "high growth" for a moat (growth without barriers is the most dangerous setup); (3) static evaluation — moats must be stress-tested dynamically. Ask: "If a competitor with $10 billion came to my market specifically to take share, how much could they take?"

Decision Checklist
  • Can this company raise prices 10% without losing customers?
  • How much money and how many years would a new entrant need to replicate its core assets?
  • Has ROIC stayed above 15% over the past decade?
  • Are technological change or regulatory shifts narrowing the moat?
  • Is management widening the moat — or quietly drawing it down?
English Insight

"The most important thing to me is figuring out how big a moat there is around the business." — Warren Buffett, 1999

Reflection This Week
Pick the highest-ROIC holding in your portfolio and write down the source of its moat — mechanistically, not as a brand name. Then imagine three scenarios in which that moat narrows. Can you rebut them?
PRINCIPLE 03

The Parable of Mr. MarketThe Parable of Mr. Market

Market psychology
Statement of the Principle
The market is your servant, not your master. Mr. Market quotes a price every day; your job is to use his emotional extremes, not catch them.
Source

Benjamin Graham, The Intelligent Investor (1949), Chapter 8. Buffett restated it in his 1987 letter and called it "the most important metaphor in the history of investing."

"Mr. Market is there to serve you, not to guide you. It is his pocketbook, not his wisdom, that you will find useful. If he shows up some day in a particularly foolish mood, you are free to either ignore him or to take advantage of him, but it will be disastrous if you fall under his influence." — Berkshire 1987 Letter, paraphrasing Graham
Deeper Reading

Graham personifies the market as a manic-depressive partner who knocks on your door every day offering to buy or sell. In good moods he names absurdly high prices; in despair he sells you out for next to nothing. The key insight: the quote is an option, not an obligation. Short-term moves reflect other participants' emotions and liquidity needs, not changes in intrinsic value. Keep "price" and "value" separate, and market swings turn from risk into opportunity. This is why Buffett insists "volatility is not risk" — directly at odds with the Beta concept in modern finance.

Classic Case

On October 16, 2008, with the S&P 500 down 38% from year-start, Buffett published "Buy American. I Am." in The New York Times, disclosing that his personal account was actively buying US equities while fear was at its peak. That same month Berkshire put $5 billion into Goldman Sachs preferred (10% dividend plus warrants) and $3 billion into GE. By 2013, the two deals had generated roughly $3 billion in combined profit. Mr. Market handed out gifts in panic; Buffett caught them.

Counterexamples and Limits

"Using volatility" is not "buy whatever drops." Common misuses: (1) treating value traps (genuinely deteriorating fundamentals) as bargains — sometimes Mr. Market is right; (2) trying to catch the bottom without a valuation anchor; (3) overtrading, turning "use volatility" into "be driven by volatility." Graham's prerequisite is that you have already independently estimated intrinsic value. Without that anchor, you cannot use Mr. Market — he uses you. Also note: extreme volatility may signal a liquidity crisis (think Lehman week, 2008), in which case cash itself becomes the scarce asset.

Decision Checklist
  • Before checking the price, have I independently estimated an intrinsic-value range?
  • Is the price drop caused by changed fundamentals or by sentiment?
  • If the market closed for three years, would my decision change?
  • Am I responding to Mr. Market's quote, or being infected by his mood?
  • Do I have pre-written rules — "add below X, trim above Y"?
English Insight

"Be fearful when others are greedy, and greedy when others are fearful." — Warren Buffett, 1986 Letter

Reflection This Week
Recall a buy or sell decision you made in the past year. Were you acting on a "price change" or a "value change"? Had you been forced to act only on intrinsic value, would you have made the same call?
PRINCIPLE 04

Intrinsic Value vs PriceIntrinsic Value vs Price

Valuation philosophy
Statement of the Principle
Price is what you pay; value is what you get. The whole craft of investing is finding a wide enough gap between the two — the margin of safety.
Source

Buffett has used these formulations many times; the most quoted version is in the 2008 letter, while the definition was set down in the 1994 Berkshire Owner's Manual.

"Intrinsic value can be defined simply: It is the discounted value of the cash that can be taken out of a business during its remaining life. Price is what you pay; value is what you get." — Berkshire 1994 Owner's Manual & 2008 Letter
Deeper Reading

This is the final output of Buffett's framework. Intrinsic value is not a precise number but a range — "It is better to be approximately right than precisely wrong." Three points: (1) value comes from future free cash flow, not accounting earnings or narrative; (2) it must be discounted — time and risk priced in via a discount rate that contains opportunity cost; (3) "remaining life" forces you to think about business longevity and terminal value. When price falls sufficiently below value (the margin of safety), both return and risk improve — Graham's most important bequest to value investing, and the reason Buffett rejects Beta as a measure of risk: the real risk is "paying too high a price for an overvalued business."

Classic Case

Washington Post, 1973: Buffett bought roughly 9.7% of the company for about $10.6 million, implying a market value of around $100 million. His intrinsic-value estimate was $400–500 million, a margin of safety above 75%. The market was depressed by the oil crisis, Watergate, and pessimism about newspapers. By the time Berkshire exited in 2013, the position had returned roughly 100×. By comparison, cheap-looking cigar-butt stocks of the same era performed nowhere near as well — the key difference was compounding intrinsic value, not a one-time discount.

Counterexamples and Limits

Common misuses: (1) equating "low P/E" with "value" (P/E is a relative metric, not intrinsic value); (2) using an artificially low discount rate to manufacture a phantom margin of safety; (3) forcing a DCF onto unforecastable businesses (loss-making growth stocks, top-of-cycle resource stocks); (4) applying "margin of safety" to businesses whose fundamentals have already materially deteriorated (the value trap). Buffett himself called the IBM investment a misjudgment — he mistook "accounting stability" for "value stability." The intrinsic-value method only works on businesses whose ten-year cash flows are forecastable.

Decision Checklist
  • Can I write down a range (not a point) for intrinsic value?
  • What is the discount to the lower bound of that range? At least 30%?
  • Does my discount rate reflect a real opportunity cost (long-bond yield plus a risk premium)?
  • If future cash flow comes in 30% below my estimate, is there still a margin of safety?
  • Am I anchoring "cheap" on business quality, or on the hope of price rebound?
English Insight

"It is better to be approximately right than precisely wrong." — Warren Buffett (paraphrasing Keynes)

Reflection This Week
Pick the position you know best. Without looking at the stock price, write down what you believe its intrinsic-value range is — and the key assumptions behind it. Then compare with the current quote: would you be willing, at this price, to become the company's sole owner?
